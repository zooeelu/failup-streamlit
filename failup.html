Welcome to FailUp

A searchable repository for negative, null, and inconclusive biomedical research findings.

Why do failed experiments matter?
In biomedical research, negative and null results are rarely published — even though they carry enormous scientific value. This is called publication bias, and it means the research community keeps unknowingly repeating the same failed experiments, wasting time and funding that could go toward genuinely new directions.

FailUp exists to fix that. By giving researchers a dedicated place to deposit, summarize, and share negative findings, we turn isolated failures into a shared, searchable body of scientific knowledge. Every null result you contribute helps someone else avoid a dead end.
How FailUp works
📤
1. Submit your finding
Upload your study documents or fill in the structured form. Our AI reads your materials and generates a standardized IMRAD-style summary — you review and approve it before it's published.
🤖
2. AI summarizes & classifies
Claude classifies the failure mode (target validity, underpowering, dosing, etc.), checks for contradictions with prior positive literature, and tags the study with ontology-based graph labels.
🔗
3. Connects to the graph
Your finding is added as a node in the Failure Graph — linked to related trials by shared mechanisms, targets, and populations. Clusters reveal structural failure patterns across the literature.
Where to go from here
◈ Dashboard →
See recent submissions, active failure clusters, and platform-wide statistics. Your starting point for exploring what's already in the repository.
↑ Submit a Finding →
Deposit a negative, null, or inconclusive result. Takes ~5–10 minutes. You'll receive a citable DOI and contribute to the shared knowledge graph.
⬡ Failure Graph →
Explore the network of failed trials. Each node is a study; connections show shared mechanisms, targets, or populations. Bigger nodes = cluster hubs with many linked failures.
◎ Search →
Search by drug target, mechanism, therapeutic area, or failure pattern. Find out if someone has already tried what you're considering — and what happened.

Failure Graph

A visual map of how failed trials connect — by shared mechanisms, drug targets, and patient populations.

📊 What this graph shows
Each circle (node) represents a submitted study. The lines (edges) between nodes mean those studies share something in common — the same drug target, biological mechanism, or patient population.

When many studies cluster together and all failed, that's a signal: this target or approach may have a structural problem that goes beyond any single trial. The graph makes those patterns visible at a glance.

Larger nodes are cluster hubs — studies that share connections with many others. Glowing nodes have contradiction flags, meaning a prior positive result exists somewhere in the literature.
🖱️ How to use it
• Filter by area using the chips below — narrow to oncology, neurology, etc.
• Click any node to open that study's full summary
• Hover a node to highlight its direct connections
• Drag to pan around the graph
• Contradiction nodes (green) indicate a conflict with prior positive literature — worth investigating
• Submit your own finding to add a node to this graph

How the NF-index works
Analogous to the h-index, your NF-index is the largest number n such that you have published n negative findings that have each been cited at least n times within FailUp.

A citation occurs when another researcher's submission references your finding as prior evidence, a failed comparator, or a contradiction. It rewards impact of negative work, not just volume.
